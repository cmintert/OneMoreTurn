## The Setting

### The Frontier Era (T+0 to T+150 years)

Humanity and its allied species have recently achieved stable FTL travel. The galaxy before us is vast and mostly unmapped. We are not the first civilization to reach the stars—there are ruins, relics, and derelict stations scattered across known space. Some are millennia old. Some are still warm.

The age of careful exploration is over. The age of claim-staking has begun.

**Timeline:**

- T-50: First FTL breach. Fragile jumps, terrible casualties.
- T+0: Reliable FTL achieved. The founding races establish their first colonial charters.
- T+50: The Great Expansion. Hundreds of settlement ships launched.
- T+100: Independent colonies begin trading and feuding.
- *Present day (T+150):* Player era. Wild frontier. Unclaimed worlds. Forgotten technology. Conflict.

---

## The Factions

The galaxy is not carved into clean territories. Instead, factions control **nodes and corridors**—the systems they've colonized, the trade routes they defend, the asteroid belts they mine. Territory is sparse. Alliances are temporary. Distrust is the default state.

Players begin with one founding factions, each occupying different regions of space and shaped by the resources available to them.

### The Central Tension

All factions want the same things: resources, territory, security. But they're shaped by different geographies and different histories, which leads to different strategies and different relationships with the unknown.

## Space & Geography

### The Known Galaxy

Space is organized loosely into **Sectors**, each containing **Star Systems**. Each system may have **Planets**, **Moons**, **Asteroids**, and anomalies.

**Navigation:**

- FTL jumps connect star systems, not arbitrary points in space.
- Jump routes form a network. Not every system is connected directly; you may need to island-hop.
- Some systems are natural hubs (high connectivity). Others are dead-ends or isolated clusters.
- Charting new jump routes is possible but risky (anomalies, pirates, the unknown).

**Scale:**

- A sector is roughly 100 light-years across.
- A system is 10–50 AU in radius.
- Travel times within a system depend on ship speed and fuel.

### Planets & Resources

**Planet Types:**

- **Terrestrial (rocky):** Can support agriculture, mining. Generic but reliable.
- **Ice worlds:** Frozen, hostile; good for specific resources (deuterium, rare metals).
- **Gas giants:** Cannot colonize directly; harvestable resources from upper atmosphere.
- **Barren:** Lifeless, mineral-rich; expensive to colonize.
- **Paradise:** Rare, fertile, high growth; every faction wants it.
- **Toxic:** Actively hostile; slow growth, hazardous mining. Resource-rich but dangerous.
- **Ruin worlds:** Abandoned settlements, derelict tech, mysteries. Salvageable but unpredictable.

**Resources:**

- **Food:** Produced by population; necessary to keep colonies growing.
- **Minerals:** Raw ore; used for ship construction and infrastructure.
- **Fuel (Deuterium):** Necessary for FTL jumps and engines.
- **Rare Earths:** Advanced ship components, tech upgrades. Scarce and fought over.
- **Knowledge (Research Points):** Extracted from ancient sites and through research facilities.

**Infrastructure:**

- Planets develop over time (small outpost → colony → major settlement → hub world).
- Each level unlocks production bonuses and new actions.
- Infrastructure can be sabotaged or destroyed by hostile forces.

### Anomalies

Not all of space is empty or predictable. **Anomalies** are regions where physics behaves strangely, where time moves wrong, or where something is still *operating* after millennia.

**Types:**

- **Nebulae:** Dense clouds of gas and dust. Sensors go blind. Ships move slowly. Excellent for hiding. Also excellent for **something** to hide in you. Contact with the nebula's fringe causes mild neural degradation in exposed crew.

- **Spatial Distortions (Pulsars, Black Holes, Rogue Singularities):** Regions where spacetime warps. FTL jumps near them are unpredictable—you may emerge 100 AU from your target, or 100 light-years. Or you may never emerge. The math says it's possible to navigate them, but no one has done it successfully twice.

- **Derelict Stations:** Ancient installations, some from the Precursor era, some far older. Most are sealed. Some respond to hails—but not with answers. Some broadcast music or mathematical sequences. The few that have been boarded are never the same after. The crews speak of *wrongness*—doors that shouldn't fit their hinges, gravity that points in multiple directions at once, rooms that are larger inside than outside. Most derelicts eventually open themselves. Nobody knows why.

- **Graveyards:** Regions dense with the wreckage of dead ships. Centuries of wrecks, sometimes millennia, layered on top of each other. Salvageable if you're brave or desperate. Ships that linger here report equipment malfunctions and odd sensor readings. Crews develop paranoia. Some don't leave.

- **The Quiet Zones:** Regions of space where nothing exists—no stars, no planets, no dust. Sensor ranges are infinite here. So is the silence. Ships that spend too long in a Quiet Zone report that their logs have gaps. Hours are missing. Crews don't remember entering them.

- **Energy Storms:** Electromagnetic phenomena that tear at ships and disrupt systems. But there's a pattern to them. They seem to avoid certain regions. They seem to hunt. Something is orchestrating them.

- **Signal Sources:** Broadcasts originating from deep space, no apparent source. Some broadcast language no one recognizes. Some broadcast coordinates. Some broadcast the sounds of breathing. Following them is optional. Nobody who has followed them and reported back was the same person who left.

- **Artifact Fields:** Scattered remnants of something vast. The artifacts are arranged in patterns, not at random. Some are warm to the touch despite being in hard vacuum. Some are colder than the cosmic background. Harvesting them is slow and dangerous—and once you've taken one, you understand why the field was abandoned. The artifacts are *part of something*. Removing one causes the others to move.

**Flavor:** Anomalies are not obstacles. They are **occupied**. The galaxy isn't empty—it's haunted by the remnants of failures, the machinery of extinct ambitions, and the spaces between spaces where things that aren't quite matter drift and wait.

## What Crews Report (And Why They Don't Last)

### The Long Dark

A human lifetime is short. A journey between stars is long. Crews on exploration vessels report:

- **Time distortion:** Clocks disagree. Some run fast, some run slow. Which one is right? The log doesn't match the crew's memories. Weeks are missing. Days repeat.
- **Sensory degradation:** Sight goes first, then hearing. The darkness of space starts to *feel* present, like it has weight. Like it's watching.
- **Equipment failure:** Not malfunctions. Failures where the equipment does something it was never designed to do. A communications array broadcasts internal thoughts. A navigation computer calculates coordinates that don't exist but somehow feel real.
- **Crew instability:** Isolation breeds paranoia, but that's not quite what happens. Crews develop shared delusions with perfect internal consistency. By the time the ship makes port, they're convinced they saw something, and all of them saw the same thing, even though they were in different parts of the ship.

Turnover in exploration is brutal. Most crews don't re-enlist. The ones who do either go mad or stop being quite human.

### The Silence Problem

When a ship is in FTL transit, it is deaf and dumb. No signals come through. No transmissions go out. For the crew, there is no time—a jump that takes 1 hour to an observer is instantaneous to the crew inside.

But sometimes, crews remember things that didn't happen. They recall conversations, repairs, problems solved—all while they were in FTL. Some ships' logs show evidence of activity during jumps that should have been impossible.

A few ships have returned with crews who were *older* than the duration of the jump would allow.

### Why We Keep Going

Despite everything—the missing time, the failed expeditions, the crews that come back wrong—we keep sending ships. Because:

1. **The resources are there.** A single Precursor artifact can fund a colony. Rare earths hidden on dead worlds are worth the risk.
2. **We don't have a choice.** The founding worlds can't sustain the population growth. Expansion is survival.
3. **We're running out of time.** There's something in the data—in the Precursor archives, in the Architect warnings—that suggests we're on a deadline. We're not sure what for. But the deadline is accelerating.

## FTL & Travel

### FTL Mechanics (Narrative Flavor)

FTL jumps are **not instantaneous**. They require:

1. **Approach Phase (1 turn):** Ship moves to the jump point at the edge of the system.
2. **Jump Phase (1 turn):** FTL drive charges and fires. Ship is in transit, visible to no one.
3. **Arrival Phase (1 turn):** Ship emerges at the target system and needs time to orient.

This creates **delay**—strategic decisions are made with incomplete information. A fleet ordered to jump cannot be recalled mid-jump.

**Fuel:**

- Every jump consumes deuterium based on distance.
- Farther jumps consume more fuel. Some routes are too far for standard ships.
- Refueling at planets extends range. Running out of fuel is catastrophic.
- Some factions are more efficient (Vorn engineers optimize fuel use).

**Navigation:**

- Known routes are safe (charted, predictable).
- Unknown routes are risky (anomalies, spatial hazards, dead ends).
- Scouting unknown routes is a legitimate action (gather intel, risk losses).

## Conflict & Coexistence

### The War (Which Never Quite Ends)

Technically, the factions are not at war. Treaties exist. Trade continues. But the fundamental conflict is insoluble.

Sooner or later, it gets violent. Not because anyone wants war. Because the math doesn't work any other way.

### Violence (Combat)

When shooting starts, it's usually not a surprise. It's the endgame of failed diplomacy.

Fleets can engage in combat. Combat is:

- **Declarative:** You order a fleet to attack. Combat resolution is automatic and deterministic.
- **Losses-based:** Ships are destroyed, disabled, or captured. Resource-intensive to replace.
- **Consequence-driven:** Attacking another faction has *permanent* diplomatic repercussions. Breaking a trade agreement costs you dearly. Attacking an ally shifts the political landscape. Other players notice and adjust.

**Fleet Composition:**

Ships come in types, each with different roles:

- **Corvettes:** Fast, cheap, fragile. Harassing and screening.
- **Frigates:** Balanced fighters. The backbone of any fleet.
- **Destroyers:** Tank damage. Heavy armor, slow.
- **Cruisers:** Rare, expensive, devastating. Command ships.
- **Transports:** Carry supplies, cargo, troops. Vulnerable but necessary.

Each faction favors different compositions based on doctrine and available resources.

### Diplomacy & Trade

Most interactions never involve combat. Trade, espionage, cold war, and slow-burning political pressure are cheaper than open conflict.

**Trade:**

- Sell resources to other factions for profit.
- Establish trade agreements (recurring deals). Breaking them damages your reputation permanently.
- Factions with strong trade ties are less likely to fight (but not impossible).
- Trade routes are vulnerable. A fleet can blockade a route and choke off another faction's income.

**Alliances:**

- Formal agreements to not attack each other (for now).
- Shared intel (limited visibility into ally's territory).
- Joint operations possible (coordinate attacks, mutual defense).
- Breaking an alliance is a declaration of war in everything but name.
- Other factions *will* exploit the chaos.

**Espionage & Subterfuge:**

- Spy networks, sabotage, industrial espionage.
- Covert action to slow rivals without direct conflict.
- If discovered, espionage is a *casus belli*—justification for war.
- Deniability matters. It's better if no one can prove it was you.

**The Politics:**

Every action you take sends a signal to other factions:

- Aggressive expansion = you're preparing for war.
- Massive trade deals = you're consolidating power.
- Sudden silence = you're planning something.
- Alliances = you're choosing sides.

Other players will respond accordingly. Trust is rare. Paranoia is rational.

## The Fracture (Why Everything Falls Apart)

### Distance is Sovereignty

A colony that's three jumps away from the core doesn't *really* answer to central authority. Communication delay means you can't enforce orders. Economic independence means you can't leverage trade. Military distance means you can't project power.

So colonies become *independent entities*. They trade with whoever pays best. They ally with whoever provides security. They drift away.

For the Inner Worlds Coalition, this is a constant drain. Outer colonies are always on the edge of secession. The Skal Ascendancy accepts this as natural—strong colonies are loyal colonies. The Vorn Conclave engineers loyalty through careful planning and genetic compatibility, but even they know that distance erodes control.

### Resource Scarcity as Political Wedge

There's enough to survive. There's never enough to thrive without taking it from someone else.

This creates **permanent strategic tension**:

- A system with abundant fuel is worth fighting over.
- A rare earth deposit changes the balance of power.
- A single Precursor artifact can fund a colony—or destabilize an alliance (who gets it?).

Players will negotiate, betray, and go to war over *things*. Not out of malice. Because the math doesn't work without them.

### The Independent Frontier

Far from core worlds, in the sparse asteroid fields and distant systems, independent operators exist. Not aligned with any faction, but trading with all of them.

These **independent colonies** are:

- Harder to control (nobody can police the frontier).
- More resourceful (you learn to make do).
- More cynical (you've seen all three factions betray promises).
- More dangerous (you can't appeal to faction loyalty—you're on your own).

Some players might try to establish independent settlements. It's risky. No faction will defend you. But you answer to no one either.

## Victory Conditions

(Deferred until Phase 4, but flavored now.)

The game doesn't end when one player dominates militarily. Victory is multifaceted:

- **Conquest:** Control X% of known space.
- **Economic:** Accumulate Y wealth through trade and production.
- **Scientific:** Unlock all technologies first.
- **Cultural:** Achieve diplomatic supremacy (most alliances, least betrayals).
- **Exploration:** Chart the entire map; discover all anomalies.

A player can win by any one condition. Conditions unlock at different game phases, so early conquest doesn't guarantee late-game victory.

## The Dead Civilizations (Background Lore)

### The Architects (Millions of Years Ago)

The oldest ruins scattered across known space defy explanation. The **Architects**—if that's what to call them—reached the stars so long ago that geological processes have worn their megastructures down to unrecognizable shapes. What remains:

- Vast structures of unknown purpose, partially submerged in planetary crust or drifting in asteroid fields.
- Mathematical markers that seem less like astronomy and more like **warnings**. Repeated glyphs carved into stone. Patterns that make your eyes hurt if you look too long.
- Scattered technology so advanced it might as well be magic. It *works*, but not reliably. Sometimes it works in ways you didn't intend.

Nobody knows what happened to the Architects. The question isn't "are they gone?" It's "what did they know that we don't?"

**The Unsettling Truth:** Their final structures are all oriented outward—away from the galactic center. Pointed toward the void. Shields? Warnings? Tombs?

**Flavor:** The Architects are not a mystery to solve. They are a threat you don't understand. Studying them is necessary and terrible.

### The Precursors (10,000 Years Ago)

A civilization closer to current human levels flourished, then **vanished in what appears to have been a very short time**. Precursor sites are found in two states:

1. **Sealed and Silent:** Vaults that have not been opened in millennia. The air inside is still breathable. The lights still work. But nothing moves. Nothing answers.
2. **Destroyed:** The scattered remains of megastructures, blown outward from their centers as if something erupted from within.

Salvaging Precursor tech is profitable but unsettling. Their ships are too elegant, too efficient—as if designed by someone who understood something crucial about spacetime that we don't. Their weapons work perfectly. Their medicine is indistinguishable from poison until it's too late.

Some sites contain **archives**—crystalline data storage that contains their history. Reading them is optional. Most crews wish they hadn't.

**The Pattern:** The Precursors left messages. The same message, repeated across dozens of dead worlds, in languages that predate anything we recognize:

"The space between stars is not empty. It is patient."

**Flavor:** The Precursors didn't die of old age or in nuclear fire. They were interrupted. By what, the records don't clearly say—only that it was sudden and that they were *afraid*. Their technology suggests they understood what was coming. It didn't help.

## The Map & Discovery

The game begins with a partially explored galaxy. Some systems are known and charted. Others are rumors and dangerous unknowns.

**Exploration:**

- Players send scout ships to unmapped systems.
- Discovering a new system reveals what's there (planets, resources, anomalies).
- Anomalies can be surveyed for more detail or left mysterious.
- Discovery is first-come, first-served. Mapping a system before a rival gives strategic advantage.

## Flavor Details & Tone

### The Frontier Is Rough

- No central authority. No laws except what you enforce.
- Pirate factions exist (NPCs, not players). They prey on weak convoys.
- Lost colonies sometimes go radio silent. What happened? Disaster? Mutiny? Invasion?
- The unknown is genuinely dangerous. Not everything is a puzzle—some anomalies kill you.

### Technology Is Messy

- Different races use different design philosophies.
- Mixing tech from different sources can be inefficient (but tempting).
- Innovation is incremental. No magic breakthroughs; steady progress.
- Some Precursor tech is so advanced you don't fully understand it. You use it, and it works, but the details are inscrutable.

### Community & Loneliness

- Colonies feel remote. Supply chains matter. A world cut off from trade suffers.
- Fleets are command structures. You know the crews by reputation.
- Encounters with other players (trade, alliance, war) break the loneliness.
- PBEM pace means decisions are thoughtful. You have time to roleplay a commander thinking about their choices.

## Narrative Hooks (For Future Expansion)

- **The Silence:** A region of space where all signals stop. Something is there.
- **The Rift:** A wormhole of Precursor origin. Where does it lead?
- **The Distress Signal:** A Precursor station broadcasting after 10,000 years of silence. Why now?
- **The Splinter:** A radical faction of your own species breaks away and founds a rival empire. Civil war or negotiation?
- **First Contact:** You detect a signal from beyond known space. Peaceful? Hostile? From where?

## Summary: The Game's Personality

**One More Turn** is a game about:

- **Expansion into the unknown.** You're colonizing a galaxy that's mostly empty but *occupied by something*. Not in a hostile way—just present. Watching.
- **Delayed consequences.** Orders take time. Decisions are made with incomplete information. By the time you know what happened to your fleet, weeks have passed.
- **Emergence over narrative.** The story is what happens when players collide, but also what they discover when they're alone. The galaxy generates its own horrors.
- **Respect for scale.** Space is big. Travel is slow. Time moves wrong out there. Empires grow, not despite the darkness, but *around* it.
- **The cost of progress.** Every resource gained has a cost you don't fully understand. Every discovery raises more questions. The deeper you dig, the more you realize you're not exploring—you're *uncovering*.
- **Dread as atmosphere, not mechanic.** The cosmic wrongness isn't a puzzle to solve. It's a fact of the setting. You learn to live with it. Most crews don't.
